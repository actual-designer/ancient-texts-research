## 1. Epic of Atrahasis — Dating

- **Claim**: "Epic of Atrahasis, c. 2450 BCE cuneiform tablet"
- **Source file**: audit/analyses/atrahasis.md
- **Verification**: DISPUTED
- **Evidence**: The oldest known manuscripts of the Atrahasis epic date to the Old Babylonian period (c. 18th–17th century BCE). A copy from the reign of Ammi-Saduqa (c. 1647–1626 BCE) is among the earliest attested. The Metropolitan Museum holds a Neo-Babylonian copy (7th–6th century BCE). The composition is generally dated to the Old Babylonian period (~1700 BCE), not 2450 BCE. The 2450 BCE date may reflect the period of the narrative events (pre-diluvian/deep antiquity) rather than the date of the cuneiform tablet itself. The INDEX table more accurately states "Pre-diluvian (deep antiquity)."
- **Notes**: The claim as written implies the tablet dates to 2450 BCE, which is not supported by manuscript evidence. Recommend revising to "Old Babylonian period (c. 1700 BCE)" for the tablet date, or clarifying that 2450 BCE refers to the narrative setting.

## 2. Epic of Atrahasis — Structure

- **Claim**: "7 chapters, 13 pages"
- **Source file**: audit/analyses/atrahasis.md
- **Verification**: UNCLEAR
- **Evidence**: The original Atrahasis epic is structured on three cuneiform tablets (Tablets I–III), not seven chapters. The "7 chapters, 13 pages" likely refers to the specific PDF used in the archive, not the ancient text's structure. The standard scholarly edition (Lambert & Millard, 1969) organizes the text across three tablets.
- **Notes**: The "7 chapters, 13 pages" appears to be a description of the archive's source PDF, not the ancient work. This is internally consistent but could mislead readers into thinking the original text had seven chapters.

## 4. Book of Ezekiel — Event Dating

- **Claim**: "events dated to 593 BCE"
- **Source file**: audit/analyses/ezekiel-merkabah.md
- **Verification**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Multiple scholarly sources confirm Ezekiel's first vision is dated to the 5th year of Jehoiachin's exile, which corresponds to 593/592 BCE (Ezekiel 1:1–2). The book contains 13 precisely dated oracles spanning 593–571 BCE. Britannica, Bible.org, and the Society for Old Testament Study all confirm this dating. Irenaeus and other early sources also support this timeline.
- **Notes**: Some sources give 592 BCE for the initial vision (based on month calculation differences), but 593 BCE is the more commonly cited date and is well within the accepted range.

## 6. Ancient Egyptian Pyramid Texts — Unis Dating

## 7. Rig Veda — Translation and Structure

- **Claim**: "*Rig Veda*, trans. Ralph T.H. Griffith (public domain), 750 pp. 1,205 hymns across 10 Mandalas"
- **Source file**: audit/analyses/rig-veda.md
- **Verification**: DISPUTED
- **Evidence**: Ralph T.H. Griffith's translation of the Rig Veda (2nd edition, 1896) is confirmed as a legitimate public-domain translation. The Rig Veda is confirmed to have 10 Mandalas (Books). However, the standard scholarly count of hymns (suktas) in the Rig Veda is **1,028**, not 1,205. The Griffith translation on sacred-texts.com and IntraText confirms 10 books with hymn counts of: Book 1 (191), Book 2 (43), Book 3 (62), Book 4 (58), Book 5 (87), Book 6 (75), Book 7 (104), Book 8 (92 + 11 Valakhilyas), Book 9 (114), Book 10 (191) = 1,028 total (including Valakhilyas).
- **Notes**: The claim of 1,205 hymns is incorrect. The correct count is 1,028. This may be a confusion with the number of verses (mantras), which totals approximately 10,552, or with some other enumeration. Recommend correcting to 1,028 hymns.

## 8. Rig Veda — Composition Date

- **Claim**: "~1500-1200 BCE (composition)" (from INDEX table)
- **Source file**: audit/00-INDEX.md
- **Verification**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The Rig Veda's composition is conventionally dated to c. 1500–1200 BCE by mainstream scholarship, corresponding to the early Vedic period. This dating is based on linguistic analysis, archaeological correlations, and astronomical references within the text. Some scholars push the date earlier (to ~1900 BCE) or later (to ~1000 BCE), but 1500–1200 BCE represents the widely accepted consensus range.
- **Notes**: The dating remains debated, but 1500–1200 BCE is the standard scholarly range.

## 10. Yoga Sutras — Structure

- **Claim**: "*Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali*, trans. Yogi Madhvācārya / Michael Beloved (2007); 4 chapters, 196 sūtras"
- **Source file**: audit/analyses/yoga-sutras.md
- **Verification**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: Wikipedia and the Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy confirm the Yoga Sutras are divided into 4 padas (chapters): Samadhi Pada (51 sutras), Sadhana Pada (55 sutras), Vibhuti Pada (56 sutras), and Kaivalya Pada (34 sutras), totaling 196 sutras (some traditions count 195). ## 15. Book of Revelation — Dating

- **Claim**: "The Book of Revelation, King James Version (41 pages); events traditionally dated to ~95 CE"
- **Source file**: audit/analyses/revelation.md
- **Verification**: CONFIRMED
- **Evidence**: The traditional dating of Revelation to c. 95 CE is well-supported by early church tradition. Irenaeus (c. 180 CE) states the vision was seen "toward the end of Domitian's reign" (Domitian ruled 81–96 CE). Eusebius places it in the 14th year of Domitian (c. 95 CE). Most modern scholars accept a date of c. 90–96 CE, with 95 CE being the most commonly cited. The KJV is confirmed as the translation used.
- **Notes**: Some scholars argue for an earlier date (c. 68–69 CE, during Nero's reign), but the traditional dating to ~95 CE remains the majority position.
